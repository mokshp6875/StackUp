Simple truth

Idle money is dying money — put it to work

Here's a concept that once you understand it, you can't unsee it: every dollar sitting in your checking account doing nothing is losing value every single day. Inflation is quietly eating it. Meanwhile, that same dollar in the market is making more dollars while you sleep, eat, and go to school.

You don't need thousands of dollars to start. Most brokerages today let you buy fractional shares — meaning you can own a piece of a $400 stock for $5. There's no "minimum" to get started. The best time to invest was yesterday. The second best time is today. Even $20 a week adds up to over $1,000 a year — which, compounded over decades, changes your life.

Think about it this way: If someone offered you a job where you earned money 24/7, never needed breaks, and got better every year, you'd take it immediately. That's what the market is. Your money goes to work so you don't have to later.

The basics

Why a savings account is holding you back

Most teens (and adults) think a savings account is the safe, smart move. And sure, it's better than hiding cash under your mattress — but not by much. The average savings account pays you around 0.5% interest per year. That means if you put $1,000 in, you walk away with $1,005 after a full year. Exciting, right?

Meanwhile, the S&P 500 — an index of the 500 biggest companies in America — has averaged about 10% per year historically. That same $1,000 becomes $1,100. Over 10 years, the savings account gives you roughly $1,050. The market? Around $2,590. Same money. Completely different life.

The hard truth: Inflation runs at about 3% per year, meaning your savings account is actually losing purchasing power every year. Your $1,000 today can buy less stuff in a year even if you didn't touch it.

The market

What the S&P 500 actually returns

The S&P 500 is the single best investing tool most people will ever need — and it's embarrassingly simple. You buy one index fund (like VOO or SPY), and you instantly own a tiny piece of 500 of the most powerful companies on Earth: Apple, Microsoft, Amazon, Google, and 496 more. When they make money, you make money.

Over the last 100 years, the S&P 500 has returned an average of about 10% per year before inflation, or about 7% adjusted for inflation. It's gone up. It's gone down. It crashed in 2008 and in 2020. Every time, it came back stronger. Time in the market always beats timing the market — meaning the longer you hold, the more it works for you.

Best move for teens: Buy VOO (Vanguard S&P 500 ETF) or SPY on any brokerage app, set it to auto-invest monthly, and don't touch it for 10+ years. That's it. That's the strategy.

Safe money

CD vs. SGOV — which is actually better?

When people don't want market risk but hate the garbage rates of a savings account, they turn to CDs (Certificates of Deposit) or treasury funds like SGOV. Both are super safe — but they work very differently, and choosing the wrong one can cost you.

A CD is like a locked savings account. You give the bank your money for a set period (3 months, 1 year, 5 years), and they pay you a higher rate — usually around 4-5% right now. The catch? Your money is completely locked up. Pull it early and you lose a chunk of interest as a penalty. Great if you know you won't need the money.

SGOV is a Treasury bill ETF — basically the U.S. government borrowing your money short-term. It pays a similar rate to CDs (around 4-5%), but your money stays completely liquid. You can sell SGOV any trading day and have cash in your account within two days. No penalty. No lock-up.

The verdict: SGOV almost always wins for teens because flexibility matters. If a stock drops 30% and you want to buy more, your money in SGOV is available. Money in a CD is stuck.

Big picture

How interest rates control everything

Here's something nobody teaches in school but everyone in finance lives by: interest rates control the entire economy. The Federal Reserve (basically America's banker) sets a base interest rate that ripples through everything — your car loan, your mortgage, your investments, even how companies grow.

When rates are high (like 5%), safe options like SGOV and CDs pay well, so some money flows out of stocks and into bonds. The stock market tends to struggle a bit. But when rates drop, those safe options pay almost nothing — so everyone rushes into stocks, pushing prices up. This is why the Fed cutting rates is almost always great news for the market.

For you as a teen investor, the key lesson is this: when rates are high, park your non-investing cash in SGOV and get paid while you wait. When rates drop, put more into index funds to ride the surge. You don't have to predict the future — just understand the pattern.

Account types

Roth IRA vs. brokerage — the biggest decision you'll make

This might be the most important financial lesson in this entire guide. There are two main ways to invest: inside a Roth IRA or inside a regular brokerage account. Both let you buy the same stocks and funds. The difference is the tax treatment — and over 30 years, taxes will be the single biggest cost you face as an investor.

A Roth IRA lets you invest money you've already paid taxes on. Then it grows completely tax-free, forever. When you withdraw at retirement, you pay zero taxes — even if you turned $10,000 into $200,000. The limit is $7,000 per year, and you must have earned income (a job) to contribute. As a working teen, this is the single greatest gift available to you. Open one immediately.

A brokerage account has no limits, and you can take money out anytime — but every gain is taxed. Sell a stock for profit and the government wants their cut. Use a brokerage after you've maxed your Roth IRA each year.

Priority order: (1) Roth IRA first — max it every year. (2) Then any leftover investing money goes into a brokerage. Never the other way around.

Stock picking

Picking stocks — how the pros find winners

Picking individual stocks is fun, but you should only do it with a small portion of your money — maybe 10%. Here's why: even professional hedge fund managers, with billions of dollars and entire teams of analysts, beat the S&P 500 only about 10% of the time. The market is incredibly hard to beat. Index funds should be your core holding.

That said, understanding how to find good stocks is a valuable skill. When looking at a company, ask: Does it make real money? Does it have more cash than debt? Is it in a growing industry? Does it have a "moat" — meaning something competitors can't easily copy? Companies like Apple, Costco, and Microsoft have dominated for decades because their customers are loyal and competitors can't touch them.

To research a stock, look up its ticker on Yahoo Finance. Check the P/E ratio (price-to-earnings) — under 25 is reasonable. Check revenue growth over 5 years — you want consistent increases. Check free cash flow — if the company generates tons of cash, it can pay dividends, buy back stock, or reinvest in itself.

Teen rule: 80-90% in S&P 500 index funds. 10-20% max in individual stocks you've actually researched. Never buy something because it's "trending" online — that's how people lose money.

Your paycheck

Exactly how much should you invest if you have a job?

If you have any income — summer job, part-time work, babysitting, whatever — here's the framework used by financially smart people at every income level. It's called the 20/20/50/10 split, adapted for teens.

Put 20% straight into investments (Roth IRA first, then brokerage). Put another 20% into savings for emergencies — 3 months of your expenses minimum, held in SGOV or a high-yield savings account. Use 50% for real needs: phone bill, gas, food, things you actually require. The last 10% is yours to spend guilt-free on whatever you want. This system works because it pays your future self first, before lifestyle creep can eat it all.

The magic number: $200/month invested starting at age 16 becomes roughly $1.1 million by age 65 at historical S&P 500 returns. Starting at 25 instead? You'd need $500/month to hit the same number. Time is worth more than money.

The secret weapon

Compounding — why starting at 16 beats starting at 25

Compound interest is the closest thing to a superpower in personal finance. Here's how it works: you earn returns on your money, and then you earn returns on those returns. It snowballs. Slowly at first, then so fast it's almost hard to believe.

Say you invest $1,000 at 10% per year. Year one you earn $100. Year two you earn $110 — because now you have $1,100 invested. Year three you earn $121. It keeps accelerating. The number seems small early on, but after 30 years that $1,000 has become over $17,000. You didn't invest more money. You just waited.

Every year you delay investing is a year of compounding you can never get back. A 16-year-old investing $50/month for 10 years, then stopping completely, will out-earn a 26-year-old investing $50/month for 30 years straight — because of the head start. That's not a typo. Starting matters more than amount.

Decision guide

What account should you use for what?

Different money serves different purposes. Using the wrong account for the wrong job is one of the biggest mistakes people make. Here's a simple guide to match your money to the right home based on when and why you'll need it:

Emergency fund
High-Yield Savings
Money you might need instantly — like if your car breaks down or you lose your job. Use a HYSA (high-yield savings account). Access it same day, no risk, earning around 4-5%.
Leftover savings
CD (Certificate of Deposit)
Money you don't need for 1-2 years and know you won't touch. Lock it in a CD for a higher guaranteed rate. Great for saving toward a car or future purchase.
Investing reserve
SGOV Treasury ETF
Cash you want available to deploy into the market when opportunities arise. Earns 4-5% while you wait, fully liquid with no penalty. The smart investor's "dry powder."

The rule is simple: emergency money needs instant access, so savings. Certain-future-purchase money earns more locked in a CD. And investing reserve lives in SGOV so it's earning and available at the same time.

Financial literacy terms — Guide edition

Every term used on this page, defined in plain English so you actually understand what you're investing in.

High-Yield Savings Account (HYSA)
A savings account that pays a much higher interest rate than a normal bank account — typically 4–5% vs. 0.01–0.5%. Offered by online banks like Marcus, Ally, or SoFi. Still FDIC-insured and safe.
CD (Certificate of Deposit)
A savings product where you lock your money with a bank for a fixed period (3 months to 5 years) in exchange for a guaranteed higher interest rate. Early withdrawal usually incurs a penalty.
SGOV
An ETF (exchange-traded fund) that holds short-term U.S. Treasury bills. It pays around 4–5% annually, is extremely safe (backed by the U.S. government), and is fully liquid — you can sell it any trading day.
Hedge Fund
A private investment fund for wealthy investors that uses complex strategies — short selling, leverage, derivatives — to try to "hedge" against losses and beat the market. Most require $1M+ to invest and charge high fees. Studies show 90% underperform the S&P 500 long-term.
Interest Rate
The percentage charged on borrowed money, or paid on savings/investments. Set at the macro level by the Federal Reserve. A high rate means borrowing costs more but safe savings pay more. A low rate makes borrowing cheap but savings earn little.
Compound Growth
Earning returns on your returns, not just your original investment. If you earn 10% on $1,000, you have $1,100. Next year you earn 10% on $1,100 — not $1,000. This snowball effect is the most powerful force in personal finance. Time multiplies it dramatically.
Index Fund / ETF
A fund that tracks a market index (like the S&P 500) by holding all the stocks in it. Instead of picking individual stocks, you own a tiny piece of hundreds at once. Low cost, diversified, and historically outperforms most active fund managers.
Roth IRA
A retirement account that grows completely tax-free. You invest money you've already paid taxes on, and all gains are yours tax-free forever. Limit: $7,000/year. Must have earned income. The single best account for teens to open.
Brokerage Account
A taxable investment account with no contribution limits and no restrictions on withdrawals. You can buy and sell stocks, ETFs, and funds freely — but gains are taxed. Use after maxing your Roth IRA.
Dividends
A portion of a company's profits paid directly to shareholders, usually quarterly. Some stocks pay 1–5% of their share price annually as dividends. Reinvesting dividends (DRIP) accelerates compound growth significantly.
Inflation
The gradual increase in prices over time — typically 2–3% per year in the U.S. It erodes the purchasing power of cash sitting in low-yield accounts. If your money doesn't grow faster than inflation, you're getting poorer in real terms even if the number looks the same.
P/E Ratio (Price-to-Earnings)
A way to measure if a stock is expensive or cheap relative to its profits. Calculated as share price ÷ annual earnings per share. A P/E under 25 is generally reasonable; over 40+ can signal overvaluation. Context matters — compare within the same industry.

Key Rules for Teen Investors

1
Open a Roth IRA first. If you have earned income, open one this week. Fidelity and Schwab have custodial accounts for minors.
2
Invest before you spend. Automate a transfer to investments on payday — before you can spend it.
3
Never try to time the market. Invest the same amount every month regardless of what the market is doing.
4
Keep 3-6 months expenses in savings. Never invest your emergency fund — you'll sell at the worst time.
5
Don't sell when markets drop. Downturns are sales on stocks. Keep buying. Time recovers everything.
6
Reinvest all dividends. Turn on DRIP (dividend reinvestment) so dividends automatically buy more shares.

Banking basics

Credit vs. debit cards — use the right one

Most teens use a debit card because it feels safer — you can only spend what you have. Makes sense. But here's the thing: credit cards, when used responsibly, are actually the better tool almost every time. They offer fraud protection, cash-back rewards, travel points, and most importantly — they build your credit score.

Your credit score is a number between 300 and 850 that determines whether you get approved for apartments, car loans, mortgages, and even some jobs. It takes years to build and seconds to destroy. The single best way to build credit young is to get a starter credit card, use it for things you'd buy anyway (gas, groceries), and pay the balance in full every single month.

Stolen card — this is huge: If your credit card is stolen and used for unauthorized purchases, you are NOT responsible. The credit card company can investigate, cancel the fraudulent transactions, and issue a chargeback — you pay nothing. A debit card is completely different. That's your actual money. Someone drains your account and the bank may take weeks to investigate (if they help at all). In many cases, you simply lose that money. This alone is a massive reason to use a credit card for most purchases.

The trap with credit cards is the interest rate — most charge 20-29% APR on any unpaid balance. If you carry a $1,000 balance, you're paying $200+ in interest per year. That's brutal. The rule is simple: never charge more than you have in your checking account, and always pay the full balance on time.

⚠️ Credit card debt is the most dangerous debt there is. It is the most common form of debt in America and the hardest to escape. Why? Because at 20–29% APR, your balance grows faster than almost any investment can outpace it. A $2,000 balance at 24% APR, paying only minimums, can take over 10 years to pay off and cost you $3,000+ in interest alone. Millions of adults are stuck in this trap right now. Do not join them.
🔴 NON-NEGOTIABLE RULE: ALWAYS PAY YOUR CREDIT CARD BILL IN FULL, ON TIME, EVERY SINGLE MONTH. Not the minimum. Not most of it. All of it. Set up autopay for the full statement balance so you never miss. One missed payment starts the debt spiral. The interest compounds. It snowballs. It becomes a burden that follows you for years. Treat your credit card like a debit card — only spend what you already have, and pay it off completely every month.
Use credit when
Credit Card
  • You can pay it off monthly
  • You want to earn rewards/cash back
  • Building your credit score
  • Online purchases (better fraud protection)
  • Stolen card — transactions can be cancelled, you owe nothing
Use debit when
Debit Card
  • You're prone to overspending
  • ATM cash withdrawals
  • You don't yet have the discipline to pay monthly
  • Splitting bills with friends
Pro tip: Get a secured credit card at 18 with a $300-500 limit. Use it for one recurring subscription (like Spotify). Set autopay to full balance. Your credit score will be 750+ within 2 years — and that will save you tens of thousands in lower interest rates on future loans.

Big purchases

How to finance a car without getting wrecked

A car is the first major purchase most people make — and most people get completely taken advantage of. Dealerships are professional negotiators. You are not. Here's what you need to know before you ever walk onto a lot.

First, understand that a car loan is always worse than paying cash. If you finance a $20,000 car at 7% interest for 60 months, you'll pay about $3,800 in interest — money that goes straight to the bank, not toward the car. The goal is to minimize the loan amount and loan length, and to get the lowest interest rate possible. Your credit score determines that rate.

Second, cars depreciate. A new car loses about 20% of its value the moment you drive it off the lot. A car that's 2-3 years old has already taken that hit — meaning someone else paid for that depreciation. Buying a 2-3 year old certified used car is almost always the smarter financial move than buying new.

1
Get pre-approved from your bank or credit union before visiting a dealership. This gives you a rate to compare against the dealer's offer.
2
Know your total price budget, not monthly payment. Dealers will stretch your loan term to make a higher price feel affordable. Focus on total cost.
3
Put at least 20% down to avoid being "upside down" on the loan — owing more than the car is worth if it gets totaled.
4
Never finance for more than 48 months on a used car or 60 on a new car. Longer terms mean more interest paid and more risk.
5
Factor in total cost of ownership: insurance, gas, maintenance, registration. These often exceed the loan payment itself.
Teen money rule: Your total car costs (payment + insurance + gas) should never exceed 15% of your take-home income. If it does, you're car-poor — and car-poor people can't invest.

Long-term goals

How buying a house actually works

Buying a home is the largest purchase most people ever make, and most people walk into it completely unprepared. Understanding how it works — even as a teenager — gives you a massive head start because you can start building toward it years in advance.

Here's the basics: to buy a home, you get a mortgage (a loan from the bank secured by the house). You typically need a down payment of 10-20% of the purchase price. On a $250,000 home, that's $25,000-$50,000 you need saved up front. Then you make monthly payments (principal + interest) over 15 or 30 years. The interest rate on your mortgage — again, determined by your credit score — will determine how much you actually pay. A 1% difference in rate on a $250,000 mortgage costs or saves you over $50,000 over 30 years.

Unlike a car, a house typically appreciates — it gains value over time. That's why homeownership builds wealth for most people. But it comes with costs: property taxes, homeowners insurance, HOA fees, maintenance (budget 1% of home value per year). Renting isn't always "throwing money away" — sometimes it's the smarter move while you save and invest.

1
Build your credit score to 740+ starting now. This gets you the best mortgage rates available.
2
Save for a down payment in a HYSA or short-term CDs — you need this money safe and accessible, not in the stock market.
3
Keep your debt-to-income ratio low. Banks want your total debt payments to be under 43% of monthly income. Student loans, car payments, credit cards all count.
4
Get pre-approved before house hunting. This tells you exactly what you can borrow and shows sellers you're serious.
5
Always get a home inspection. Skipping this to save $400-600 can cost you tens of thousands in undisclosed problems.
Start now: The habits you build at 16-18 — credit score, saving rate, debt avoidance — will determine whether you can buy a house at 25 or 35. The earlier you understand this, the more options you have.

Adult basics

Taxes — what you need to know as a teen with a job

If you have a job, you pay taxes. Period. The federal government takes a percentage of your income through income tax, and you also pay Social Security and Medicare taxes (called FICA) — about 7.65% automatically. Your employer withholds this from each paycheck, which is why your take-home pay is less than your hourly rate times hours worked.

Every January you'll get a W-2 from your employer showing what you earned and what was withheld. You then file a tax return by April 15. If too much was withheld, you get a refund. If not enough, you owe money. As a teen with a part-time job, you'll likely get most of your withholding back as a refund — but you still need to file. Use free tools like IRS Free File or TurboTax Free Edition.

The most important tax concept for teen investors: capital gains. When you sell an investment for more than you paid, the profit is taxed. But if you hold it for over a year, you pay the long-term capital gains rate — often 0% if you're in a lower income bracket. This is another reason why the Roth IRA is so powerful: zero taxes on gains, ever.

Quick rule: Hold investments for at least one year before selling to qualify for long-term capital gains rates (usually 0% or 15% vs. your regular income tax rate, which is higher). And always, always file your taxes — even if you think you don't owe anything.

Financial literacy terms — Life Finance edition

Every term from this page, explained clearly so you know exactly what you're dealing with in the real world.

APR (Annual Percentage Rate)
The yearly interest rate on a loan or credit card, expressed as a percentage. If your credit card has a 24% APR and you carry a $1,000 balance for a year, you'll owe $240 in interest on top of the original debt. This is why carrying a balance is dangerous.
Credit Score
A number between 300 and 850 that represents how trustworthy you are as a borrower. Lenders use it to decide whether to approve you for loans and what interest rate to charge. Factors: payment history (35%), amounts owed (30%), length of credit history (15%), new credit (10%), credit mix (10%).
Mortgage
A loan from a bank or lender used to buy a home, where the home itself is collateral (the bank can take it if you stop paying). You typically repay a mortgage over 15 or 30 years. The interest rate you get is heavily influenced by your credit score — a 1% difference can cost or save you $50,000+.
Loan
A sum of money borrowed from a lender that must be repaid with interest over time. Common types include student loans, auto loans, and personal loans. Each loan has a principal (the amount borrowed), an interest rate (APR), and a repayment term (how long you have to pay it back).
Down Payment
An upfront payment made when purchasing a car or home. For homes, lenders typically require 10–20% of the purchase price as a down payment. A larger down payment reduces your loan amount, lowers monthly payments, and often gets you a better interest rate.
Debt-to-Income Ratio (DTI)
The percentage of your monthly income that goes toward debt payments (student loans, car payments, credit cards, etc.). Lenders want this under 43% before approving a mortgage. The lower your DTI, the more financial flexibility you have and the better loan terms you'll receive.
Depreciation
The decrease in an asset's value over time. Cars depreciate rapidly — losing about 20% of their value the moment you drive off the lot, and around 50% in the first 3 years. Unlike homes, cars almost never appreciate. This is why buying used is often smarter financially.
Capital Gains Tax
A tax owed when you sell an investment for more than you paid. Short-term gains (held under 1 year) are taxed as regular income — up to 37%. Long-term gains (held over 1 year) are taxed at 0%, 15%, or 20% depending on your income. Holding investments longer saves you real money.
FICA Taxes
Payroll taxes that fund Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%), totaling 7.65% of your gross paycheck, automatically withheld by your employer. These are separate from income tax and are not refundable — they go directly to fund government benefit programs.
W-2 Form
A tax form your employer sends you each January showing your total earnings for the prior year and how much was withheld for federal and state taxes. You use this to file your annual tax return. If you had multiple jobs, you get a W-2 from each employer.
Pre-Approval
A lender's conditional commitment to loan you money up to a certain amount, based on a review of your credit, income, and finances. Getting pre-approved before car shopping or house hunting gives you negotiating power and shows sellers you're a serious buyer.
Chargeback
When a credit card company reverses a transaction — cancelling a charge and returning the money to your account. This is the mechanism that protects you when your card is stolen or fraud occurs. Debit cards have much weaker chargeback protections, which is another reason credit cards are safer for purchases.
